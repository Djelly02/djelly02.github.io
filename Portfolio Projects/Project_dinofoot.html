Dinofoot: Ostrich-Inspired Multi-Segmented Robot Feet

As part of the Integrated Project in the Master of Mechanical Engineering at KU Leuven, I worked in a team of four students under the supervision of Prof. Badri-Spröwitz. Our task was to investigate a self-chosen research question that was testable and built as an extension of BirdBot, a tendon-driven bipedal robot developed by our professor. BirdBot achieves high efficiency compared to fully motor-actuated robots by storing energy in spring tendons during the stance phase and releasing it back into the system at toe-off.

We decided to explore the impact of an ostrich-inspired multi-segmented robotic foot as an extension of BirdBot. The aim was to investigate how different foot designs influence locomotion dynamics and shifts in the center of pressure (COP).

Project Objective

Most bipedal robots rely on simple or rigid feet, which limits their locomotion efficiency. Our project explored how introducing two-segment toe designs could enhance gait stability and energy efficiency by dynamically shifting the COP during stance.

The position of the COP beneath the robot’s foot determines how energy is injected into the system. Energy can be directed into the body, causing oscillations of the center of mass (COM), or into the swing phase of the leg, resulting in faster leg swings and reduced COM oscillations. Our goal was to investigate how the COP influences this energy injection and whether different foot designs could alter this distribution.

To achieve this, we set out to design, build, and experimentally validate novel foot prototypes for the original BirdBot design.

Approach

  • Research: Conducted a literature review on ostrich and emu biomechanics and tendon-driven robotic legs. Defined research questions and assessed feasibility given time constraints.
  • Design: Designed and 3D-printed two alternative two-segment toe mechanisms with distinct cam geometries. The redesigned robot, named StrutBot, incorporated these features.
  • Sensor implementation: Integrated load cells and camera-based motion tracking to measure tendon forces, COP shifts, and joint kinematics. The test setup included synchronization of multiple sensor systems for accurate post-processing. An Arduino was used to read load cell data and trigger the camera with a light source.
  • Control: Adapted existing motor control and schematics from BirdBot to operate StrutBot.
  • Prototyping: Implemented Dynamixel actuators, 3D-printed body parts, and custom joints, along with novel tendon tensioning mechanisms to replicate biological flexion–extension behavior.
  • Testing: Conducted treadmill-based experiments to capture gait dynamics under controlled conditions.

Results

  • Built and tested two robotic foot prototypes with different joint cam radii, producing distinct COP trajectories during stance.
  • Demonstrated that increasing the radius of the proximal cam led to earlier lifting of proximal segments, dynamically shifting the COP toward distal segments.
  • Showed that COP shifts correlated with changes in energy injection patterns, highlighting the potential of proper foot design for more efficient and versatile locomotion.
  • Identified areas for improvement in data collection methods and mechanical design for future iterations.

Reflection

This project strengthened my skills and passion for mechatronics, experimental robotics, and biomechanics-inspired design. Working on the full development cycle required balancing CAD design, mechanical prototyping, electronics integration, and experimental validation. The results emphasized how subtle design choices can significantly influence locomotion dynamics in robotic systems.
